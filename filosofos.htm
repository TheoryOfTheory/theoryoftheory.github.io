Why?
* The assessment of where the best / most important philosophical work was being done during a given period tend to correlate quite strongly with our assessment of where the best / most important scientific work is being done during the same period. (How much of this is our biases and how much of it is real?) So the standard Enlightenment narrative goes very rougly something like "starts in Italy, moves north into Switzerland, the low countries, and France, eventually reaches its pinnacle in England and Germany." You see approximately the same narrative in the transition from medieval to early modern philosophy: start with the Rome-associated Scholastics, move north into France with Descartes, Malebranche, etc. and end up in England and Germany with Locke, Leibniz, Hume, Kant, Mill, Hegel. So the question I would ask would be something like: why does Spain get left out of both of these stories?

* antropologo cubano Fernando Ortiz's transculturacion
* Influenced by the Marxist criticism of Georg Lukács , Galvano Della Volpe , Arnold Hauser , and Karl Mannheim ; the critical theory of the Frankfurt School of Theodor Adorno and (most especially) Walter Benjamin ; and the structuralism of Claude Lévi-Strauss and Michel Foucault , to which we must add Latin American anthropologists such as Darcy Ribeiro or Fernando Ortiz , Rama's purpose consisted of coining a theoretical apparatus that, on the one hand, provided a form of analysis " Latin American", which combined the critical tradition of the continent with the new currents brought from Europe, on the other hand tried to put that singularity in relation to the other literatures of the world. In this sense, although he was not the first to reflect on the condition, nature or Latin American literature (figures such as Pedro Henriquez Ureña , Alfonso Reyes or Mariano Picón Salas had already done so ), he was perhaps the one who dealt with these the most.
* Rama exemplifies transculturation with careful readings of authors such as José María Arguedas , Juan Rulfo , and João Guimarães Rosa .

Walter Mignolo* https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Mignolo#Libros
* En 1995 publica su libro The Darker Side of the Renaissance, que lo dio a conocer mundialmente en el campo de los estudios poscoloniales y subalternos, así como en el ámbito de la Filosofía latinoamericana, debido a sus constantes referencias a la obra de filósofos como Edmundo O'Gorman, Rodolfo Kusch, Leopoldo Zea y Enrique Dussel. En este libro, Mignolo sostiene la tesis de que el renacimiento europeo de los siglos XV y XVI tuvo "otra cara" olvidada e invisibilizada: la colonización de las Américas. Esta se convertiría en una de las tesis centrales del Grupo modernidad/colonialidad, en cuya formación Mignolo cumplió un papel clave. El grupo estaba conformado por figuras de la intelectualidad académica latinoamericana como Aníbal Quijano, Enrique Dussel, Arturo Escobar, Santiago Castro-Gómez, Edgardo Lander y otros. Sus escritos nutrieron buena parte del trabajo colectivo del grupo
